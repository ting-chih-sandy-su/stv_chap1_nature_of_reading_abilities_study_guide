📌 Chapter Overview & Road Map (p.3)

▼

The chapter provides an exploratory map of reading, building a case that reading is far more complex than everyday definitions suggest.

1. Definition of Reading→ 2. Purposes for Reading→ 3. Fluent Reading (10 features)→ 4. How Reading Works (processes)→ 5. Models of Reading
📝 Scope NoteThe chapter focuses on fluent L1 reading as the foundation. L1 vs. L2 differences are addressed in Chapter 2. At very advanced levels, L1 and L2 reading abilities tend to merge.

1. Why a Simple Definition of Reading Fails (pp. 3–4)

▼
📖 The Simple (Insufficient) Definition "Reading is the ability to draw meaning from the printed page and interpret this information appropriately."
💡 Main Argument — 5 reasons this definition is inadequate
  1. Purposes: Does not convey that there are multiple ways to engage in reading, each requiring different skill/strategy combinations
  2. Skills complexity: Does not reveal the many skills, processes and knowledge bases acting in combination and in parallel
  3. Time constraints: Does not explain reading as a cognitive process under intense time constraints — yet these are essential to fluent comprehension
  4. L2 proficiency: Does not highlight how meaning-drawing varies with L2 proficiency
  5. Social context: Does not address the social context of reading or why texts are interpreted/used differently
These 5 reasons are the most likely opening question on a quiz. Shorthand: Purposes → Complexity → Time → L2 → Social.

2. Why a Book on L2 Reading Starts with L1 (p.4)

▼
💡 4 Reasons for focusing on L1 reading research
  1. Far more research has been carried out on reading in L1 contexts (especially English as L1)
  2. L1 students usually achieve a reasonable level of fluency; same cannot be said for L2 learners
  3. Ability to draw implications for instruction — including training studies — is much more developed in L1 contexts
  4. L1 reading instruction has been a source of many instructional innovations not yet explored extensively in L2 contexts
📝 AnnotationEven if many L2 students will never become fully fluent readers, they can be taught to make as much progress as possible. L1 reading research provides the target end-point. At very advanced levels, L1 and L2 reading abilities appear quite similar.
A common exam question: "Why do Grabe & Stoller start with L1 research in a book about L2 reading?" — 4 reasons above.

3. Purposes for Reading — Section 1.1 (pp. 5–9)

▼
💡 Main Argument Reading decisions are made rapidly and almost unconsciously. Different purposes activate different skill combinations. 7 main purposes are identified (heuristic categories — many variations possible).
#PurposeKey FeaturesTypical Context
1Search for simple infoScan for specific word/number/phrase; slow down to verify. Some see it as an independent cognitive process (Guthrie & Kirsch, 1987)Phone directories, indexes
2Skim quicklySample segments; guess where important info is; use basic comprehension on key segments until general idea formsNewspapers, text previewing
3Learn from textsRemember main + supporting ideas; build rhetorical frames; link to knowledge base; slower rate; stronger inferencing demandsAcademic/professional contexts
4Integrate informationSynthesise across sources; decide relative importance; restructure rhetorical frames; requires critical evaluationResearch, multi-source essays
5Read to writeSelect, critique, compose from text — a task variant of reading to integrateEssays, reports, papers
6Critique textsEvaluate and judge — another task variant of reading to integrate. Both #5 and #6 require selecting, critiquing, composing.Academic critique tasks
7General comprehension ⭐ MOST FUNDAMENTALUnderstand main ideas, not necessarily all details; relate to background knowledge; underlies all other purposesNovels, articles, everyday reading

Carver's "Reading Gears" (Quote 1.1, p.5)

Quote 1.1 — Carver (1997, pp. 5–6) Five reading processes (gears): scanning (Gear 5) → skimming (Gear 4) → rauding (Gear 3) → learning (Gear 2) → memorising (Gear 1). Rauding — normal, ordinary, natural reading — is the most common. Sharon (1973) surveyed 5,067 adults and found less than 1% of their reading involved anything difficult to understand during their typical 2 hours/day of reading.
📝 AnnotationRauding (Gear 3) = general reading and listening rate. The most typical adult reading. Used for magazines, newspapers, novels, memos, letters from friends. Key stat: <1% of daily reading is difficult.

Background Knowledge (Quote 1.2, p.6)

Quote 1.2 — Hudson (2007, p. 293) Knowledge of any text topic is essential for successful comprehension regardless of the language. Without basic knowledge, it would be impossible to approximate a writer's intended message. Background knowledge cannot be ignored when attempting to understand reading comprehension.

Why General Comprehension is Saved for Last (pp. 8–9)

📝 Important Nuance General comprehension is intentionally discussed last for two reasons:
  1. It is the most basic purpose, underlying all others
  2. It is more complex than commonly assumed — "general" does not mean "simple"
Because of its processing efficiency demands, general comprehension may at times be harder to master than reading to learn — a common misperception exists because of how these are tested in schools.
Know all 7 purposes AND be able to explain why general comprehension is both the most fundamental AND potentially the most complex.

4. Skills, Strategies & Reading Processes (pp. 8–10)

▼

🔧 SKILLS

Definition Linguistic processing abilities that are relatively automatic in use and combination. Acquired gradually, eventually automatised (Anderson, 1995; Proctor & Dutta, 1995; Schunk, 2000).

Examples: word recognition, syntactic processing

🧠 STRATEGIES

Definition Abilities potentially open to conscious reflection; reflect a reader's intention to address a problem or goal while reading (Anderson, 2009).

Examples: predicting, summarising, rereading, critiquing the author

The Blurred Distinction (Quotes 1.3 & 1.4, pp. 9–10)

Quote 1.3 — Alexander and Jetton (2000, pp. 295–6) Two key differences between skillful and strategic processing: automaticity and intentionality. Skills are routinised, automatic academic habits. The same procedure (e.g. finding main idea) can be either a skill or a strategy depending on whether the reader consciously evokes it or functions automatically.
Quote 1.4 — Afflerbach, Pearson and Paris (2008, p. 17) Paris et al. (1991) described reading strategies as "skills under consideration" — the same actions could be either a skill or a strategy, depending on the reader's awareness, control, intention, and the specific reading situation.
💡 Key Argument The distinction between skills and strategies is not entirely clear — and this is due to the nature of reading itself, not a definitional problem. Many abilities identified as strategies are relatively automatic for fluent readers (e.g. skipping unknown words, rereading). Skills may have been learned as strategies but become thoroughly automatised.
🔑 Key Term — Reading Processes Cognitive activity involving skills, strategies, attentional resources, knowledge resources, and their integration.
🔑 Key Term — Abilities General term covering comprehension skills, strategies and knowledge resources available to the reader.

5. Defining Fluent Reading — 10 Features, Concept 1.3 (pp. 11–13)

▼
💡 Main Argument No single process defines reading comprehension by itself. Together these 10 features provide an accurate account (Concept 1.3). They must be understood as a set, not individually.
#FeatureKey Details
1RapidGood L1 readers: 200–300 words per minute. More rapid = better processing component operation.
2EfficientProcesses must be coordinated; some must be automatic (Breznitz, 2006).
3InteractiveTwo ways: (a) processes run simultaneously; (b) text information interacts with background knowledge from long-term memory.
4StrategicReader recognises processing difficulties, addresses imbalances, makes decisions for monitoring and shifting goals (Pressley, 2006).
5FlexibleAdapts to changing purposes and ongoing comprehension monitoring.
6EvaluatingAssesses coherence and match to purpose. Extends to motivations, attitudes, feelings of likely success/failure, expectation of usefulness (Baker & Beall, 2009).
7PurposefulEvery reading act is triggered by an internal or external purpose or task.
8ComprehendingUnderstanding the text — both obvious (the goal) and subtle (how it is carried out).
9A learning processMost common way students learn new information in academic settings is through reading.
10LinguisticFundamentally language-based — NOT a reasoning process (rejects dominant 1980s–90s view). Without any word knowledge, there is no comprehension possible.
Memorise all 10. Mnemonic: R-E-I-S-F-E-P-C-L-L. Be ready to explain WHY reading is linguistic (not just a reasoning process) — the Chinese/Finnish example from p.13 is useful evidence.

6. Working Memory — Concept 1.4 & Section 1.3.1 (pp. 12–15)

▼
📖 Definition — Working Memory (Baddeley, 2007) Information activated or given mental stimulation for immediate storage and processing. Now generally preferred over "short-term memory."

4 components (Baddeley's model):
  • Central executive processor — controls attention; coordinates sub-systems
  • Visual–spatial sketchpad — processes visual/spatial information
  • Episodic buffer — integrates information across systems
  • Phonological loop — processes language/sound information
📊 Key Working Memory Facts Working memory keeps new information active for 1–2 seconds Lower-level process activations: brief (1–2 sec) unless rehearsed Higher-level processes: continual reactivation of main ideas throughout reading
💡 Key Argument — Speed is ESSENTIAL, not optional If processing of active information is not done quickly enough, information fades from memory and must be reactivated — using more resources and making reading inefficient. Speed is not a "nice extra" — it is a necessary component of comprehension.
📝 The Car Analogy — Concept 1.5 (p.14)
  • 🚗 The car = Reading comprehension (achieving the destination)
  • ⛽ Fuel = Word recognition (necessary but not sufficient alone)
  • 🔧 Engine = Other lower-level processes (syntactic parsing + semantic proposition formation)
  • 🏁 Reaching destination = Higher-level processes (full text understanding)
Lower-Level Processes (Concept 1.5)Higher-Level Processes (Concept 1.5)
• Lexical access
• Syntactic parsing
• Semantic proposition formation		 • Text model of comprehension
• Situation model of reader interpretation

📝 Important NoteLower-level processes are more skills-oriented and automatic. Higher-level processes make more use of background knowledge and inferencing. Neither is assumed to be easier than the other.

7. Lower-Level Processes — Section 1.3.2 (pp. 15–19)

▼

7.1 Lexical Access / Word Recognition (pp. 15–16)

📖 Definition Lexical access = calling up the meaning of a word as it is recognised. The most fundamental requirement for fluent reading comprehension.
📊 Key Statistics — Memorise These Fluent L1 readers recognise 98–100% of all words in a text Rate: 4–5 words per second ≈ 230 milliseconds per word (Pressley, 2006) Fastest recognition possible: under 100 milliseconds (<1/10th of a second) Reading to learn: ~200 wpm (Pressley, 2006, p. 51) Relaxed/general reading: 250–300 wpm
Quote 1.5 — Pressley (2006, p. 51) When reading to learn, each word is typically fixated for about a quarter of a second — about 200 wpm. Reading at a more relaxed pace occurs at 250–300 wpm.
Quote 1.6 — Pressley (2006, p. 68) Decoding and comprehension compete for short-term memory capacity. Slow word analysis consumes most capacity, leaving little for comprehension. Automatic word recognition consumes very little capacity, freeing it for comprehending words, sentences, paragraphs, and whole texts.
💡 Automaticity of Lexical Access For fluent readers, lexical access is automatic: it cannot be consciously reflected on, and it cannot be suppressed. When the eye sees a word, the reader cannot stop accessing its meaning. Requires thousands of hours of practice.
📝 L2 AnnotationWord recognition is under-discussed in L2 contexts — a mistake. Fluent L2 reading requires the same large recognition vocabulary. The challenge is giving L2 students the time, resources and practice to build it.

7.2 Syntactic Parsing (pp. 16–18)

📖 Definition Syntactic parsing = extracting grammatical information from stored words to support clause-level meaning. Involves recognising phrasal groupings, word ordering, subordinate and superordinate clause relations.
💡 Why Syntactic Parsing Matters
  • Disambiguates words with multiple meanings (e.g. bank, cut, drop)
  • Resolves pronoun and definite article references in prior text
  • Clarifies clause-level structure: who did what to whom
📊 Research Evidence (correlation with reading comprehension) L1: Klauda & Guthrie (2008) — r = .75 with 278 fifth-graders (strongest of 6 skills) L2: Alderson (1993) — r = .80 L2: van Gelderen et al. (2004) — r = .80
Quote 1.7 — Urquhart and Weir (1998, pp. 60–1) Demonstrates the necessity of syntactic parsing by showing a jumbled English sentence without function words or inflections is incomprehensible → restoring word order helps slightly → restoring function words and inflections makes the message fully extractable: "The machine begins by digitally recording the speech and passing it to several recogniser modules."
📝 Tacit vs. Explicit Grammar Knowledge L1 students often read fluently yet fail grammar exercises consciously — they know grammar tacitly but not explicitly. Proves automatic processing can exist without metalinguistic knowledge. For L2 students: the issue is not whether they need grammar knowledge, but that they need countless hours of print exposure to develop automaticity.

7.3 Semantic Proposition Formation (pp. 18–19)

📖 Definition Semantic proposition formation = combining word meanings and structural information into clause-level meaning units. Builds a propositional network of text meaning.
💡 How it works — Concept 1.6 Words active in working memory (1–2 sec) + grammatical cueing → integrated meaning units. Repeated/reactivated concepts become main ideas. Cause–effect relations and discourse markers (e.g. "however") signal larger propositional relations. The process is largely automatic — becomes conscious only when comprehension breaks down. This process anticipates the text model of comprehension (higher-level).
📝 Summary of Lower-Level ProcessesAll three — lexical access, syntactic parsing, semantic proposition formation — are relatively automatic for fluent readers and work effortlessly together in working memory. When any one fails, comprehension slows considerably.
Expect a question asking you to name and define all three lower-level processes. Know that they are automatic, brief in working memory (1–2 sec), and interdependent.

8. Higher-Level Processes — Section 1.3.3 (pp. 19–23)

▼

8.1 Text Model of Comprehension (pp. 20–21)

📖 Definition Text model of comprehension = an internal summary of main ideas built as reading proceeds. The set of main ideas the reader forms. Not to be confused with general "models of reading" discussed in Section 1.4.
💡 How the Text Model is Built New clause-level meaning units are added to a growing network by:
  • Repetition of an idea, event, object, or character
  • Reference to the same thing in different words
  • Simple inferences that link new units to the network (part–whole, subordinate–superordinate)
Ideas reactivated repeatedly → become and remain more active → viewed as main ideas.
Ideas no longer named or indirectly referred to → pruned from network and fade. What readers remember is the gist.
Quote 1.8 — Pressley (2006, pp. 53–4) During normal reading, the reader processes individual ideas but remembers the gist. Main ideas are coded sentence by sentence, held in memory, and linked to the next sentence. Bridging inferences reconcile previous and new meaning. Good readers do not make inferences randomly. Prior knowledge plays an important role in generating required inferences.
📝 AnnotationThe text model amounts to an internal summary — which is why summary-writing is a reasonable and useful learning task to practise. Inferencing required for the text model is typically not extensive unless the text is very new or language proficiency impedes comprehension.

8.2 Situation Model of Reader Interpretation (pp. 21–23)

📖 Definition Situation model of reader interpretation = the reader's personal interpretation of the text. Built on and around the emerging text model. Shaped by: background knowledge, inferences, reader goals, motivation, task, text difficulty, reader attitudes.
Quote 1.9 — Perfetti, Van Dyke and Hart (2001, pp. 133–4) Two mental models are needed: (1) text base — what the text says (essentially linguistic; propositions from sentences; inferentially poor); (2) situation model — what the text is about (essentially agnostic in form; built from text base + additional inferences; inferentially rich). Main difference: inferences — text base is inferentially poor; situation model is inferentially rich.

📄 Text Model (Text Base)

  • What the text says
  • Essentially linguistic
  • Based on propositions from sentences
  • Inferentially poor
  • → Produces a summary

🧠 Situation Model (Interpretation)

  • What the text is about (for the reader)
  • Integrates background knowledge
  • Shaped by goals, attitudes, feelings
  • Inferentially rich
  • → Produces a critique
💡 Duality of Understanding — Genre Implication The situation model explains how a reader can both summarise a text AND critique it. Different genres signal different reading approaches:
  • A poem → reader interpretation (situation model) is the goal
  • A technical manual → assumed preferred author interpretation (text model)
This explains the justification for the existence of different genres.
⚠️ Warning — Where Fluent Readers Can Go Wrong Wrong or incomplete background knowledge, or faulty inferences, can lead even a fluent reader astray — specifically at the situation model stage. This is where background knowledge is most dangerous.
📝 Background Knowledge in the Situation Model Background knowledge (as linked networks, instances of memory, schema theory, or mental models) plays a supporting role throughout reading. It helps:
  • Anticipate discourse organisation
  • Disambiguate word-level and clausal meanings
  • Interpret new information through the reader's goals, feelings and background expectations
The ability to integrate text and background information appropriately and efficiently is the hallmark of expert reading in a topical domain.

8.3 Executive Control Processing (pp. 22–23)

📖 Definition Executive control processor = an attentional monitor (aspect of working memory) that focuses selective attention, assesses understanding, evaluates reading success, shifts strategies, repairs comprehension problems.
📝 Key Distinction
  • Evaluation of text content (how we feel about the text) → part of the situation model
  • Evaluation of how well we comprehend → dependent on the executive control processor

8.4 How Purpose Determines Process Emphasis (p.23)

📝 Purpose → Process Link
  • Reading to find simple info → emphasises word recognition + background knowledge anticipation of target items
  • Reading for general comprehension → balanced combination of text model + situation model
  • Reading to learn → first build an accurate text model, then a strong situation model integrated with existing/revised background knowledge
Be able to compare and contrast the text model and situation model. Know why the situation model is where background knowledge errors cause the most damage.

9. Reading Every 2 Seconds & L2 Breakdown — Concept 1.7 (pp. 23–25)

▼
📊 Concept 1.7 — In Every 2 Seconds of Fluent Reading 1. Focus on and access 8–10 word meanings 2. Parse a clause → form a meaning unit 3. Connect new meaning unit to the growing text model 4. Check interpretation vs. purposes, feelings, attitudes, background expectations 5. Monitor comprehension; make inferences; shift strategies; repair misunderstanding 6. Resolve ambiguities; address difficulties; critique text information
💡 Three Conclusions (p.24)
  1. Reading comprehension processes work in parallel when some skills are relatively automatic
  2. Some processes must be automatic for reading to work efficiently
  3. Fast and efficient processing is the hallmark of fluent reading comprehension
⚠️ When Reading Breaks Down — L2 Context (p.24) Difficulties arise when readers lack adequate background knowledge, linguistic resources, or sufficient reading experience. L2 readers resort to two ineffective coping strategies:
  1. Slow mechanical translation → working memory efficiencies cannot operate → poor comprehension
  2. Forcing a preconceived situation model onto the text → inappropriate background knowledge activated → poor comprehension
If repeated continually → learners lose motivation to become fluent readers.

Long-range solution: Students need many hours of reading at text and task levels appropriate to their abilities. Only extended exposure to meaningful print builds fluency.
The "2 seconds" concept is very testable — it vividly illustrates why automaticity is non-negotiable. Also know the two L2 breakdown strategies and their consequences.

10. Models of Reading — Section 1.4 (pp. 25–31)

▼
📝 Important DistinctionGeneral models of reading (Section 1.4) must NOT be confused with the text model and situation model (Section 1.3) — those are comprehension process concepts, not general models.

10.1 Metaphorical Models — Concept 1.8 (pp. 25–27)

ModelCore IdeaVerdict
Bottom-up Letter → word → sentence, strictly linear, mechanical, little interference from background knowledge Extreme form inaccurate, but lower-level automatic processes reflect bottom-up aspects. Research broadly supports bottom-up over top-down.
Top-down Reader goals/expectations drive reading; sample text to confirm/reject predictions; heavy use of background knowledge and inferencing; executive control generates expectations Mostly discredited in strong form. Extreme version is self-defeating. Few researchers support strong top-down views.
Interactive Combines bottom-up and top-down Simple interactive model is self-contradictory — automatic lower-level processing is incompatible with strong top-down control. A modified interactive (hybrid) model is most accurate.
Quote 1.10 — Stanovich and Stanovich (1999, p. 29) Two decades of empirical research resolved debates in favour of bottom-up models. Greater use of context to aid word recognition is not a characteristic of good readers; developing phonological sensitivity is critical for early reading success; decoding-emphasis programmes result in better reading outcomes.
💡 Modified Interactive Model — The Most Accurate Metaphorical Model Automatic lower-level processes run mainly bottom-up with minimal top-down interference (context/background knowledge would slow processing). Top-down knowledge reserved for higher-level processing. On occasion, context disambiguates word meaning (e.g. bank, bug, dope) — but this is the exception. Some purposes (integrating across texts, skimming) do involve more top-down processing.

10.2 Specific Research-Based Models — Section 1.4.2 (pp. 28–31)

ModelAuthor(s)Core ClaimStatus
Interactive Compensatory Model Stanovich (2000) — proposed late 1970s (a) Readers develop efficient processes; (b) less-automatic processes interact regularly; (c) automatic processes operate relatively independently; (d) reading difficulties → increased interaction and compensation. When automatic processes break down, slower compensatory processes (e.g. context clues) kick in, slowing reading. ✅ Validated over 3 decades
Word Recognition Model Seidenberg & McClelland (1989); Coltheart (2005) alternative Explains fluent word recognition via connectionist / neural network theory. Neural networks reassemble with greater facility each time a word is encountered. Not a model of full reading comprehension — covers only the major input. Fundamentally bottom-up. ✅ Widely recognised
Simple View of Reading ⭐ Hoover & Gough (1990) RC = Decoding × Listening Comprehension (both as %). One of the most influential current models. Compatible with word recognition models and Interactive Compensatory Model. ✅ Most influential — ongoing debates about measurement and additional factors
Dual-Coding Model Sadoski & Paivio (2001, 2007); Sadoski (2009) Verbal and visual processing are two separate but linked cognitive systems that reinforce each other. Learning improved when visual representations match prose. Draws on Interactive Compensatory Model, Simple View, and Verbal Efficiency Model (Perfetti, 1999). Rejects abstract semantic propositions/schemata — comprehension built directly from linguistic + visual input (Paivio, 1986, 2007). Supported by multimedia learning research (Mayer, 2009). ✅ Growing in popularity
Psycholinguistic Guessing Game Model Goodman (1986, 1996) Reading = (a) hypothesise → (b) sample → (c) confirm. Classic top-down model. Claims reading is universally the same across all proficiency levels and languages; all reading abilities transfer automatically. ❌ Fundamentally wrong — rejected by Gough & Wren (1999); Pressley (2006); Stanovich (2000)
📊 Simple View of Reading — The Only Formula in the Chapter Reading Comprehension (RC) = Decoding (D) × Listening Comprehension (LC) Both D and LC measured as percentages, then multiplied. Neither alone is sufficient — both necessary. (Gough, Hoover & Peterson, 1996)
Quote 1.11 — Gough, Hoover and Peterson (1996, p. 3) Skilled reading requires skill in both decoding and comprehension. A child who cannot decode cannot read; a child who cannot comprehend cannot read either. Both are necessary; neither is sufficient.
Quote 1.12 — Alderson (2000, p. 12) Reading is essentially divided into two components: decoding (word recognition) and comprehension. Comprehension consists of parsing sentences, understanding in discourse, building a discourse structure, and integrating with prior knowledge. This comprehension process is not unique to reading — it also describes listening.
⚠️ Why the Psycholinguistic Guessing Game Model is Rejected
  1. Good readers do NOT guess — they rely on automatic word recognition
  2. Poor readers use context MORE than good readers (opposite of the model's prediction)
  3. Wrongly assumes reading is identical across all languages and proficiency levels
  4. Wrongly assumes all reading abilities transfer automatically across languages
  5. Has led to ineffective instructional practices
  6. Useful only as an account of one early stage of reading development — but Goodman never intended it that way
📝 Constructivist / Transactional Models (p.30) These related models (reader-response theories) presume readers can carry out basic reading comprehension processes rather than explaining how processes operate or develop. Quote 1.13 (Stahl, 1997) critiques response-oriented curricula: long discussions may take time away from actual reading practice, and discussion time has been negatively correlated with reading achievement (Harris & Serwer, 1966).
Quote 1.13 — Stahl (1997, pp. 22–3) Response-oriented classes encourage students to discuss individual reactions to texts. However, transporting this approach to early grades creates problems: responding presupposes one can read; excessive response can be as numbing as excessive questioning on minute details; long discussions take time from reading practice. Harris & Serwer (1966) found discussion time was negatively correlated with achievement.
Know all 5 specific models, all 3 metaphorical models, and be able to explain the Guessing Game Model rejection (6 reasons). The Simple View formula is the only equation — memorise it.

11. Conclusion — Section 1.5 (pp. 31–32)

▼
💡 Chapter's Overall Argument The chapter outlines a view of reading well supported by L1 research and compatible with L2 reading research of the past 20 years. The emphasis on individual cognitive processes is NOT intended to deny:
  • Social factors in reading development (family literacy, social group experiences, primary schooling, peer/sibling interaction around literacy events)
  • Relevance of social contexts on purpose and processes themselves (addressed in Chapter 2)
Rather: the intention is to highlight information not well known among reading teachers and raise awareness for curriculum planners and teachers.
📝 Figure 1.1 (p.32) The authors refer to Figure 1.1 — a diagram summarising all factors that must be taken into account when considering the complex nature of reading. These factors inform student needs assessment and meaningful reading instruction planning. (Note: the diagram itself is a visual — its content is captured throughout this guide.)
📝 Bridge to Chapter 2 Much of the research supporting this chapter is drawn from L1 contexts. Chapter 2 will explore differences between L1 and L2 reading — which should influence interpretation of L2 reading comprehension abilities, L2 development, and implications for reading instruction.
